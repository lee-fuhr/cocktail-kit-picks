Essential home bar tools guide

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

You only need six basic tools to make almost any cocktail at home: a shaker, a jigger, a bar spoon, a strainer, a muddler, and a citrus juicer. Skip the specialty gadgets and focus on quality versions of these essentials.

Which shaker should you choose: Boston or cobbler?

The Boston shaker is the workhorse of professional bars. It consists of a metal tin and a mixing glass (or a second tin). It seals tightly, holds more volume, and is easier to clean and maintain. The downside is that you need a separate strainer (a Hawthorne strainer) and there’s a small learning curve to breaking the seal. The cobbler shaker has a built-in strainer and a cap, which makes it more beginner-friendly and compact for small kitchens. However, the built-in strainer can clog with herbs or fruit pulp, and the lid sometimes sticks when cold. If you plan to make mostly spirit-forward stirred drinks, a Boston shaker is the better long-term choice. For fruit-heavy cocktails and occasional use, a cobbler is perfectly fine.

Why a jigger matters more than you think

A jigger is the single most important tool for consistent cocktails. Without one, you’re guessing, and guessing leads to unbalanced drinks. The classic two-sided jigger with a 1.5-ounce and 0.75-ounce cup covers most recipes. Japanese jiggers with narrow, tall cups allow for more precise pours, while the traditional hourglass shape is easier to read for beginners. Avoid multi-measure jiggers with tiny lines that are hard to see. A simple, well-marked jigger in the 1-ounce to 2-ounce range will serve you for years. A budget-friendly jigger from a reputable barware brand is a better investment than a fancy set of shot glasses.

Strainers: Hawthorne, julep, and fine-mesh

If you use a Boston shaker, a Hawthorne strainer is essential. Its coiled spring fits the tin rim and strains out ice and large solids while letting liquid through. For juleps or single-glass stirred drinks (like an Old Fashioned), a julep strainer is the classic choice, it’s a shallow, perforated spoon that sits inside the glass and holds back ice. A fine-mesh strainer is a cheap, invaluable addition. Even if your Hawthorne or cobbler strainer does its job, fine-mesh catches tiny shards of ice, herb bits, and citrus pulp. It costs very little and dramatically improves the texture of shaken drinks, especially those with muddled ingredients. Many experienced home bartenders consider it a third essential strainer.

Muddling and citrus: the fresh-fruit essentials

A muddler should be sturdy, with a flat, untextured base if you’re muddling delicate herbs, or a slightly ridged base for fruit. Avoid wooden muddlers that can splinter over time; food-safe nylon or stainless steel with a silicone head is easy to clean and durable. A muddler is really only needed for cocktails like Mojitos, Caipirinhas, or Mint Juleps. A citrus juicer is far more important. A simple reamer or a sturdy two-handle press (often called a Mexican elbow) will handle limes, lemons, and even small oranges with little effort. Fresh juice makes the difference between a good margarita and a great one. Buy a juicer that is easy to clean and fits comfortably in your hand.

Glassware that covers almost every cocktail

You don’t need a dozen glass shapes. Three or four will cover 90% of drinks: a rocks glass (Old Fashioned/lowball) for spirit-forward drinks, a highball/collins glass for tall drinks and fizzes, a coupe or Nick & Nora glass for stirred or shaken cocktails served up, and maybe a stemmed wine glass for sours or Aperol Spritzes. Thicker, dishwasher-safe glass makes sense for a home bar that sees regular use. Resist the urge to buy a huge set of matching glasses. Instead, buy two or three of each essential shape from a reputable restaurant-supply or glassware brand. They will be more durable and cheaper per glass than fancy sets, and you can replace broken ones individually.

Ice is an ingredient, not an afterthought

The quality and shape of your ice affects dilution, temperature, and presentation. For shaken drinks, standard refrigerator ice works fine, but for stirred drinks or on-the-rocks serving, larger cubes melt more slowly and keep the drink cold without watering it down quickly. A simple silicone ice mold that makes large cubes or spheres costs very little and is worth it. A Lewis bag and a mallet or a clean kitchen towel and a rolling pin can crush ice for juleps and tiki drinks. Avoid specialty ice machines until you’ve confirmed you consistently make cocktails that need crushed ice. At home, a good mold and a hand-crank ice crusher (or the towel method) will cover almost everything.

Frequently asked questions

What is the absolute minimum set of tools I need to start?

A shaker (any style), a jigger, a bar spoon, a strainer (Hawthorne if using a Boston shaker, or the built-in one on a cobbler), a muddler, and a citrus juicer. That’s six tools. Everything else (mixing glass, peeler, channel knife, speed pourer) is optional.

Can I use a mason jar as a shaker?

Yes, a wide-mouth mason jar with a lid can work for shaking, but it’s not ideal. The lid can leak, the jar is heavy, and it’s harder to strain. It’s a fine temporary solution, but a dedicated shaker is much easier to use and clean.

Do I need a mixing glass or can I use a pint glass?

A standard mixing glass (like a Yarai or a simple beaker) is nice for stirring drinks because its shape allows the bar spoon to rotate without spilling. But a sturdy pint glass or even a large measuring cup works fine. The key is having a vessel with a wide enough opening to fit a julep strainer.

How do I clean and maintain my bar tools?

Wash tins, strainers, and jiggers by hand with warm water and mild soap immediately after use, don’t let citrus residue dry. Bar spoons can go in the dishwasher if stainless steel. Wooden or coated muddlers should be hand-washed and dried completely. Dry all metal parts thoroughly to prevent spots or rust.

Should I buy a complete cocktail kit or choose individual pieces?

Buying individual pieces lets you pick quality items that suit your needs. Many pre-made kits include a cheap cobbler shaker, a flimsy jigger, and a plastic muddler that you’ll want to replace quickly. If you find a kit from a reputable barware brand with all-metal construction and solid reviews, it can be a convenient start, but individual purchases usually give better value.

What thickness of bar spoon is best?

A standard bar spoon has a twisted handle that helps with stirring and a weight at one end (or a fork/paddle at the other). A length of about 12 inches (30 cm) is sufficient for mixing glasses. Avoid very thin spoons that bend easily. A solid, moderate-weight spoon from a known brand will last.

In shortBuilding a home bar doesn’t require a huge investment or a wall of gadgets. Start with the six core tools, add good glassware and a solid ice mold, and you’ll be able to make hundreds of classic cocktails. Focus on quality where it counts, the shaker, the jigger, and the strainer, and you’ll enjoy the process of making drinks at home for years.
