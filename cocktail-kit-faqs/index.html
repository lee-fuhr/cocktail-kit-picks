Cocktail kit questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

A cocktail kit should include a shaker (Boston or cobbler), a jigger, a muddler, and a strainer, plus a bar spoon if you're shaking stirred drinks. Boston shakers are more versatile for home use, while cobbler shakers are easier for beginners. Start with a budget-friendly set and upgrade tools one at a time as you find your preferences.

What should a basic cocktail kit include?

Every starter kit needs four essentials: a shaker, a jigger for measuring, a muddler for crushing herbs and fruit, and a strainer to keep ice out of your glass. Beyond that, a bar spoon helps with stirring, and a citrus juicer or reamer is handy for fresh juice. Most pre-assembled kits include these tools, but check the list, some skip the strainer or muddler to keep the price low. If you’re buying a kit for a friend who’s just getting into cocktails, look for one that also has a mixing glass or a Hawthorne strainer. A cutting board and a small knife aren’t part of a kit, but you’ll want those nearby too. The best kits give you a solid foundation without a lot of gadgets you’ll never use.

Boston shaker vs cobbler shaker: which is better?

The Boston shaker is two pieces: a metal tin and a glass or metal mixing cup. It seals tightly, holds more ice, and gives you more control over shaking. Professionals favor it because it’s easier to clean and less likely to leak. The cobbler shaker has three pieces: a built-in strainer, a top cap, and a bottom cup. It’s simpler for beginners, you pour, shake, and strain directly, but the strainer holes can clog with fine ice or fruit pulp. For a home cocktail kit, a Boston shaker is the more versatile choice. If you’re new and want fewer parts to fiddle with, a quality cobbler shaker works fine. Just be aware that some cobbler shakers have a plastic cap that can freeze or crack over time. Many starter kits include a cobbler shaker; if you buy pieces separately, go with a Boston tin set.

Do I need a separate muddler and strainer?

Yes, a muddler and strainer are worth getting, even if your shaker has a built-in strainer. A dedicated Hawthorne strainer (metal with a spring coil) catches finer ice chips and fruit pulp that a cobbler lid’s holes might miss. A muddler is essential for mojitos, old fashioneds, and other drinks that call for muddled herbs or fruit. You can use the back of a spoon in a pinch, but a proper muddler, preferably wooden or food-grade silicone, gives better control and won’t break. Some budget kits skip the muddler and strainer entirely. If you pick a kit that includes both, you won’t have to buy them later. For the best bang for your buck, look for a kit that has at least a double-sided jigger, a Hawthorne strainer, and a wooden muddler. That trio will cover 90% of what you’ll make at home.

What material is best for cocktail shakers?

Stainless steel is the standard for shakers because it’s durable, easy to clean, and doesn’t break if you drop it. Look for a shaker made of food-grade stainless steel with a brushed or polished finish, it won’t react with acidic ingredients like citrus juice. Glass mixing cups are common in Boston shaker sets; they let you see the liquid level but can chip or shatter if knocked over. Some kits come with a copper or brass finish for looks, but those are often just plating over stainless steel and can tarnish or peel over time. For the jigger and strainer, stainless steel is again the best choice. Wooden muddlers are traditional and gentle on glassware, while silicone muddlers are dishwasher-safe and less likely to splinter. If you’re choosing a pre-assembled kit, check that the shaker’s seal is tight, twist-test it if possible. A leaky shaker is more frustrating than any material preference.

Are pre-assembled kits better than buying individual tools?

Pre-assembled kits are great for beginners because they bundle everything you need at a lower cost than buying each tool separately. Many kits also include a nice storage box or stand, which keeps your counter tidy. The trade-off is that the tools in a kit are often budget-grade, the jigger might not have clear measurement lines, the muddler could feel flimsy, and the shaker’s finish may wear quickly. For someone who makes a cocktail once a month, that’s fine. For a regular home bartender, you’ll likely replace a few pieces within a year. Buying individual tools lets you pick professional-quality versions of each item, a thick-walled Boston shaker, a weighted jigger with etched marks, a sturdy Hawthorne strainer. You can also skip the tools you don’t need. If you’re on a tight budget, start with a reputable pre-assembled kit (avoid the very cheapest ones) and upgrade the shaker or jigger later. Most people end up with a mix of kit pieces and standalone upgrades after a few months of practice.

How do I choose a cocktail kit for gifting?

For a gift, focus on the recipient’s experience level and style. A beginner will appreciate a full starter kit that includes a shaker, jigger, muddler, strainer, and maybe a recipe booklet. Look for a set with a nice box or stand, it makes a better presentation and gives the person a place to store everything. If they already have some tools, consider a specialized kit: a margarita set with a salt rimmer and citrus juicer, or an old fashioned kit with a mixing glass, bar spoon, and wooden muddler. Avoid kits that look flashy but skimp on essential pieces. A “15-piece set” might have multiple shot glasses and a cocktail pick set but lack a proper strainer. Check the description carefully. In the budget-friendly to mid-range price band, you can find solid stainless steel kits that will last for years. If you’re spending a bit more, look for weighted jiggers and a shaker with a tight seal. A personal note with a few cocktail recipes makes the gift extra thoughtful.

Frequently asked questions

What’s the difference between a Hawthorne strainer and a julep strainer?

A Hawthorne strainer has a metal disc with a spring coil around the rim, it sits inside the shaker and works for most drinks. A julep strainer is a perforated spoon-like scoop used with a mixing glass for stirred cocktails like Manhattans. Most home kits include a Hawthorne strainer, which is more versatile. If you make many stirred drinks, a julep strainer is nice to have.

Can I use a cocktail kit without a jigger?

You can, but your drinks will be inconsistent. A jigger gives you accurate measurements (common sizes are 1 oz and 2 oz or 1.5 oz and 0.75 oz). Without it, you’ll need shot glasses or measuring spoons. For a beginner, a double-sided jigger is a small investment that dramatically improves your cocktails.

How do I clean a cocktail shaker to prevent rust?

Rinse the shaker and strainer with warm water immediately after use. Wash with mild dish soap and a soft sponge; avoid abrasive scrubbers that can scratch stainless steel. Dry everything thoroughly with a towel, don’t air-dry, especially if you have hard water, because mineral spots can lead to pitting. For the rubber seal on cobbler shakers, hand wash and air dry separately.

Is a weighted jigger worth the extra cost?

Yes. A weighted jigger has a thick metal base that won’t tip over when you pour, and its interior shape lets you measure accurately without spilling. Cheaper jiggers are often thin and unstable. The extra weight also makes them feel durable in the hand. If you buy a budget kit, upgrading to a weighted jigger is one of the first smart investments you can make.

What size cocktail shaker should I look for?

A standard shaker holds about 18 to 24 ounces, which is enough for two cocktails at once. Smaller “personal” shakers (around 12 oz) are fine for single drinks but limit your batch size. If you plan to make drinks for a group, a larger Boston shaker (28 oz) or a cobbler shaker on the bigger side gives you more room to shake without leakage.

Do I need a mixing glass if I have a shaker?

Not strictly necessary, but a mixing glass makes stirred cocktails (like martinis and old fashioneds) easier and more elegant. You can stir in a pint glass or even a large measuring cup. A dedicated mixing glass with a spout helps pour cleanly and it’s usually thick-walled to keep the drink cold without cracking. Many cocktail kits now include one as a bonus piece.

In shortThe best cocktail kit is the one that matches how often you make drinks and what you like to shake. Start simple with the essentials, shaker, jigger, muddler, strainer, and don’t be afraid to swap out a piece later. Whether you buy a pre-assembled set or build your own, a well-chosen kit will make every cocktail a little more enjoyable and a lot more consistent.
